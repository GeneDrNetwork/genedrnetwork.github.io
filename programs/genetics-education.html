What is Genetic Testing and Types of Genetic Testing?

Genetic testing looks for changes in genes or chromosomes that may help explain a health condition or a person's chance of developing one.

What is Genetic Testing?

Types of Genetic Testing

Different tests answer different questions:

  • Single-gene testing examines one gene when a specific condition is suspected.
  • Gene panels examine a group of genes linked to similar symptoms or conditions.
  • Chromosomal microarray looks for missing or extra segments of chromosomes.
  • Exome sequencing analyzes the protein-coding regions of most genes.
  • Genome sequencing analyzes nearly all of a person’s DNA.
  • Targeted familial variant testing checks for a specific genetic change already identified in a family member.
What is Chromosome Microarray?

A chromosome microarray detects copy number variants, which are deletions or duplications of chromosome material. It is often used when evaluating developmental delay, autism, congenital anomalies, or other unexplained medical conditions. Unlike DNA sequencing, which reads the order of DNA letters and can identify smaller sequence changes, microarray primarily detects larger missing or extra chromosome segments.

What is Exome Sequencing?

Whole exome sequencing looks for changes in the parts of genes that provide instructions for making proteins.

What is Gene Therapy?

Gene therapy is an advanced treatment that aims to treat or prevent disease by modifying, replacing, repairing, or adding genetic material within a person's cells.

How to Read a Genetic Sequencing Test Report

A genetic sequencing report usually begins with the patient information and the test methodology, which describe whose sample was tested, the type of analysis performed, and the test’s limitations. The gene findings section lists relevant genes and variants identified by the laboratory.

Variant Classification

  • Pathogenic: the variant is known to cause disease.
  • Likely Pathogenic: the available evidence strongly suggests the variant causes disease.
  • Variant of Uncertain Significance (VUS): there is not enough evidence to know whether the variant causes disease.
  • Likely Benign: the available evidence strongly suggests the variant does not cause disease.
  • Benign: the variant is not considered disease-causing.

The interpretation section connects the findings with the patient’s medical history, while clinical recommendations may describe follow-up care, family testing, or additional evaluation. Because results and classifications can be complex and may change as knowledge advances, it is important to discuss the report with a genetics professional.

How to Understand Genetic Test Results

Positive Result

A positive result means the test found a genetic change related to the reason for testing. A genetics professional can explain what it may mean for you and your family.

Negative Result

A negative result means the test did not find the genetic change it was designed to detect. It does not always rule out a genetic cause.

Variant of Uncertain Significance (VUS)

A VUS is a genetic change that experts cannot yet classify as harmless or disease-causing. More information may become available over time.

What is a genetic disorder?

A genetic disorder is a health condition related to a change in a gene or chromosome. Some are inherited, while others happen for the first time in a person.

What are genes, DNA, and chromosomes?

DNA is the genetic material found in our cells. Genes are segments of DNA that contain instructions for how the body grows and functions. Chromosomes are structures that package DNA and genes together inside each cell.
